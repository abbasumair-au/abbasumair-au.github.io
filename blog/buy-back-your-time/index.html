Book Summary

Buy Back Your Time

September 19, 2025 · by Umair Abbas

I picked up Buy Back Your Time by Dan Martell at a point where I was doing everything myself — infrastructure, pipelines, oncall, documentation, side projects. The kind of busy that feels productive but isn't really going anywhere.

The book didn't tell me anything I hadn't heard before. But it organized the ideas in a way that actually made me act on them. Here's what stuck.

The core idea

Most people try to get ahead by working more. Martell's argument is the opposite: the real move is to buy back your time — by delegating, outsourcing, or automating anything that isn't your highest-value work.

The question he keeps coming back to is simple: Is this the best use of my time right now? It sounds obvious. But answering it honestly is harder than it seems.

The Time-Value Ladder

Not all tasks are equal. Martell breaks them down by the dollar value they generate:

$10/hr
Admin, errands, repetitive work
$100/hr
Project management, customer support
$1,000/hr
Strategy, sales, product development
$10,000/hr
Vision, unique insight, brand-building

The goal is to fire yourself from the bottom rungs as fast as possible — and keep climbing. Every hour you spend on $10 work is an hour you're not spending on $1,000 work.

The Replacement Ladder

Where to start? Martell's answer is more practical than most: begin with your personal life, not your business.

Outsource laundry, groceries, cleaning first. It feels uncomfortable — like a luxury you haven't earned. But that discomfort is exactly the point. Once you see that paying someone else $30 to clean your flat frees up two hours for actual deep work, the logic becomes hard to argue with.

Then move into your work: delegate email, calendar, admin. Build SOPs so others can do things the way you'd do them. Keep climbing until your time is spent almost entirely on the work only you can do.

The Pain Line

There's a moment in every delegation where you want to take it back. The other person does it differently. Maybe slightly worse. It feels easier to just do it yourself.

Martell calls this the pain line — and says most people stop right here. That's why they stay stuck. Pushing through isn't about lowering your standards. It's about accepting that good enough from someone else is worth more than perfect from you, when your time has better uses.

The Buyback Loop

The actual process Martell recommends is a three-step loop you run continuously:

  1. Audit — Track where your time actually goes. Not where you think it goes. Where it actually goes.
  2. Transfer — Systemize and delegate the low-value work. Write the SOP, hand it off, resist the urge to take it back.
  3. Fill — Reinvest the freed time into high-value work, or rest that makes your high-value work better.

The loop only works if the third step is intentional. Most people audit and delegate, then fill the freed time with more low-value work. That's just staying busy in a different way.

What I'm actually taking from this

The mindset shift isn't really about productivity. It's about seeing delegation as an investment rather than an expense — buying freedom and energy, not just output.

For me, in a DevOps context: this maps directly to automation. Every manual process I still run by hand is a $10/hr task I haven't delegated to a script yet. Every runbook that only exists in my head is a bottleneck I'm choosing to keep.

The book is worth reading if you're at a point where you're busy but not sure the busy is taking you anywhere. It won't tell you anything revolutionary — but it might finally make you do the obvious things you've been putting off.
